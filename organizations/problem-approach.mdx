Building infrastructure that is meant to work with government bodies and counselling authorities is different from building a product. The approach matters — not just the output.

This page describes how the team thinks about the problem and what principles guide the work.

---

## The Design Principle

Every capability in the proposed architecture is evaluated against one question: does this remove a coordination burden from the student, or does it just move it around?

A feature that improves one portal without eliminating the need for the student to manually coordinate between systems is not progress. A feature that genuinely removes a step the student currently performs manually — regardless of how technically complex that is to implement — is worth pursuing.

This also defines the boundary of the project. Superadmission is not trying to improve any individual counselling system. It is trying to eliminate the gap between systems that the student currently has to bridge alone.

---

## Design Decisions: How They Are Made

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with the operational reality">
    Every capability starts with what actually happens in the current system — not what it is designed to do, but what students and administrators experience in practice. A feature that solves a theoretical problem without addressing an observed operational one does not belong in the architecture.
  </Step>
  <Step title="State the constraint before the solution">
    Before any solution is designed, the constraint is stated explicitly. What operational burden does this address? What does it require from systems outside Superadmission's control? Features that depend heavily on external action are clearly separated from features that can be built and validated independently.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Document what is uncertain">
    Where a design decision involves genuine uncertainty — a technical assumption that may not hold, a behaviour that varies across systems — that uncertainty is documented. It is not resolved by choosing the optimistic answer. The Standards and Assumptions page in the Blueprint exists specifically for this.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## What the Project Holds as Non-Negotiable

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Accuracy over optimism" icon="check-circle">
    The documentation describes what the system is designed to do, not what it will do. No capability is stated as deployed or live unless it is. Every integration is framed as proposed or intended. This is not a communication style choice — it is the only basis on which institutional readers can trust the documentation.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Authority structures are not negotiable" icon="check-circle">
    JoSAA allocates seats for IITs, NITs, and GFTIs. MCC allocates AIQ medical seats. State CETs allocate within their jurisdiction. None of this changes. The proposed layer has no involvement in allocation decisions. This boundary is architectural and is not a position that will shift under any framing.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Student data belongs to the student" icon="check-circle">
    The student's profile, documents, and choices are not shared with any counselling system the student has not actively enrolled in. No data is used for purposes beyond the admission workflow. Consent is specific, revocable, and logged.
  </Card>

  <Card title="Regulatory compliance is a design requirement" icon="check-circle">
    DPDP, IT Act, Aadhaar regulations, and DigiLocker terms of use are built into the architecture. They are not compliance checkboxes to address before launch. A system that requires cutting corners on data governance to function at scale is not the right system.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## On Working with Government and Authorities

The project is designed for eventual engagement with counselling authorities and government bodies. That engagement requires specific things from the project that are worth stating clearly.

**Technical specificity.** A vague proposal does not survive review by NIC, authority technical teams, or ministry bodies. The architecture is documented at the level of specificity required for genuine technical evaluation — not as a concept, but as a system with defined layers, defined data flows, and defined integration points.

**Regulatory groundedness.** The project must demonstrate that it understands the regulatory environment as a set of substantive constraints, not as a checklist. The Governance and Compliance page in the Blueprint documents how the architecture addresses each relevant regulation and where open questions remain.

**Honesty about what does not exist yet.** No authority integrations exist. No government approvals are in place. No production system is running. Any conversation with an authority or ministry begins from this honest starting point. Overstating readiness — even slightly — ends that conversation.

**Respect for institutional timelines.** Government and authority processes move on their own timelines. The project is designed to make progress on the components that can be built independently while those conversations develop, not to treat government timelines as obstacles to work around.
