title: "Risks and Tradeoffs"
description: "Where the design involves genuine tensions; choices where gaining something means giving something up."
---

Every infrastructure design involves tradeoffs. The question is not whether they exist but whether the people making them are aware of them and have thought them through. This page documents the significant ones in Superadmission's design not to invite doubt, but because clarity about tradeoffs is part of building something trustworthy.

---

## Centralisation vs. resilience

Superadmission creates a shared identity and document layer across counsellings. That is its core value. It is also its primary risk: a shared layer is a shared point of failure.

If Superadmission experiences a significant outage during an active allocation window, the impact is not limited to one counselling portal. It affects every counselling that depends on the shared layer. The System Reliability section covers the architectural decisions designed to reduce this risk — horizontal scaling, asynchronous flows, graceful degradation, checkpoint-based allocation. But the risk is not eliminated. It is managed.

The tradeoff is deliberate: the coordination benefits of a shared layer outweigh the resilience cost of not having one, given the current state of the system. But it requires taking the reliability architecture seriously — not as an afterthought.

---

## Speed vs. verification thoroughness

Admissions operates on tight timelines. Registration windows open and close in days. Allocation rounds run back to back. A verification process that takes two weeks is effectively useless in a process that runs on two-week windows.

Superadmission's DigiLocker integration verifies documents at source — which is fast. But "verified at source" means "confirmed that this document was issued by this authority." It does not mean "confirmed that every field in this document is correct." A caste certificate issued by a district authority and fetched via DigiLocker is confirmed as genuine. Whether the category claimed matches the category on the certificate, and whether the certificate meets the specific requirements of a specific counselling, is a separate check that requires more processing.

The platform handles consistency checks — mismatched dates, category-document mismatches — automatically where detectable. But edge cases exist, and some of them will only surface at the time of physical joining at the college. Faster verification catches most problems. It does not catch all of them.

## Guidance vs. influence

Pravesh AI is designed to inform, not to decide. It surfaces probability estimates, comparable alternatives, and plain-language explanations of what a seat offer means. The student makes the choice.

The line between "informing" and "influencing" is real and it matters. A guidance layer that prominently surfaces certain colleges — for any reason, including algorithmic reasons that are not visible to the student — is not a neutral tool even if it does not make explicit recommendations. The design actively avoids this: the guidance layer does not rank or recommend institutions, it explains the student's own data back to them.

But the design intent and the actual effect of a system at scale are not always the same thing. How students respond to probability estimates — whether they interpret "30% likelihood" as a reason to remove a preference rather than keep it — is a behavioural question that requires live observation. The risk that guidance shades into influence is one we take seriously and will monitor actively in pilots.

---

## Privacy vs. interoperability

The more counsellings share data through a common layer, the more useful the platform becomes. It is also the more data about a student that is consolidated in one place.

Superadmission's data architecture is designed around minimisation: each counselling sees only the data relevant to that counselling's process, and the student's participation in one counselling is not visible to another except at the level of cross-counselling conflict detection. The student's complete profile — scores, preferences, documents — is not shared across counsellings.

But the layer exists, and its existence means that a data breach or a governance failure at the platform level has consequences across all connected counsellings simultaneously. The privacy protections are real. So is the risk surface that consolidation creates. These cannot both be zero.

---

## Early adoption vs. first mover risk

The first counselling authority to integrate with Superadmission takes the risk of being first. They are integrating with a system that has not run a live allocation cycle at scale. The technical risk — that something fails under real conditions that worked in testing — is real, and it falls on them and their students.

This is the classic infrastructure adoption challenge. The first mover bears risk that later adopters will not bear, because later adopters benefit from the first mover's experience. Superadmission's pilot design tries to manage this by limiting the first pilot's scope — a defined cohort, a defined set of rounds, a defined fallback if the integration fails. But the first mover risk is not eliminated. It is reduced and managed.

Finding the right first pilot partner — one with enough technical capacity to integrate, enough risk tolerance to be first, and enough belief in the value to commit — is one of the most important near-term challenges.
