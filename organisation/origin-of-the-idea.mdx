---
title: "Origin of the Idea"
description: "Superadmission did not start as a platform idea. It started as a question that kept getting harder to ignore."
---

The founding team did not set out to build admissions infrastructure. That came later and somewhat reluctantly, because it became clear that nothing short of infrastructure would actually solve the problem.

What started the work was simpler: an attempt to help students navigate a process that felt unnecessarily hard.

---

## College Cult - where it began

Before Superadmission, there was College Cult — a small initiative started by the co-founders to make admission counselling more accessible. The premise was straightforward: many students were struggling not because they lacked the merit to get into good colleges, but because they didn't know what to do after results were declared. Which counsellings to register for. What "freeze-float-slide" meant. How to read last year's closing ranks. Whether they were eligible for a second counselling they hadn't heard of.

We began by working with students directly sometimes at no cost, sometimes at a nominal fee  helping them understand their options and make better decisions. In the early stages, the impact was tangible. Students who had been paralysed by uncertainty found a path. Choices that seemed overwhelming became manageable. Several students landed seats they would likely have missed without the guidance.

That felt meaningful. And for a while, it seemed like the problem was solvable this way.

---

## What the scale revealed

The more students we worked with, the more clearly a different pattern emerged.

Every hour of guidance we provided was an hour absorbed on behalf of the student. Every form we helped fill, every deadline we tracked, every document checklist we cross-referenced none of that was information the student couldn't have found. It was information that was scattered, inaccessible, and too time-consuming to compile alone.

We were not removing complexity. We were carrying it.

And that model had a hard ceiling. The number of students we could help was limited by the number of hours we could put in. Scale the guidance effort and you scale the cost, not the infrastructure. The students who most needed help — first-generation applicants, students from smaller towns, students without the money or network to find a good consultant — were exactly the ones who were hardest to reach through a people-led model.

The question that this raised was not "how do we reach more students?" It was a different one: why does navigating this process require a guide at all?

---

## The shift in framing

That question took some time to answer properly, because the initial instinct was to attribute the complexity to information gaps. If students had better information — a cleaner comparison of colleges, a clearer explanation of the counselling process; the problem would be smaller.

That instinct was wrong.

The problem was not that information didn't exist. It was that the information was distributed across systems that didn't talk to each other, and the student was expected to do the coordination work. Register on this portal. Upload the same document on that one. Track this deadline independently of that one. Figure out, on your own, that you qualified for a counselling you hadn't registered for.

Better information about a broken process doesn't fix the process. It makes navigating the brokenness slightly more efficient.

The real question was structural: what would it take to build a system where the coordination was done by the infrastructure, not by the student?

---

## What fieldwork made undeniable

Research and direct interaction with students across admission cycles made certain things clear that desk research couldn't.

The coaching industry's "admission guidance" vertical — consultants charging families thousands of rupees to track deadlines, help fill preference lists, and explain how counselling works — was not filling a gap in the information landscape. It was filling a gap in the infrastructure. The demand for paid guidance was a direct measure of how much process knowledge the system required students to have in order to participate effectively.

Counselling authority staff, when we spoke to them, were not unaware of the problem. Many of them understood exactly why students struggled. What they didn't have was a mechanism — a shared infrastructure layer — that would let them solve the problem cooperatively across counsellings. Each authority was operating within its own boundaries, and those boundaries were not designed for the student navigating across all of them.

Institutions were spending enormous staff time on document verification that was, in many cases, re-verifying documents that had already been verified elsewhere. The redundancy was not invisible to the people running these processes. It was simply unavoidable given the architecture.

---

## The turning point

The moment the idea shifted from "a better counselling service" to "infrastructure" was specific, even if the shift had been building for a long time.

It came from drawing a parallel that, once drawn, was difficult to unsee: admissions in India in the early 2020s looked like payments in India in the early 2010s. Multiple systems. No interoperability. High friction. Heavy dependence on intermediaries to navigate what should have been simple. And a sense that this was just how things worked — that it was complex, but it was the only option.

UPI didn't improve payments. It changed what payments were. It created a shared infrastructure layer that connected every payment actor — banks, merchants, individuals — through a common protocol. The friction didn't get smaller; it got structurally removed.

That's what admissions needed. Not a better portal. A shared layer beneath all the portals.

Once that framing was clear, the question became: what would that layer actually consist of? What problems does it need to solve? Who does it need to work for? What does it need to connect?

Superadmission is the attempt to answer those questions in working software.

---

## What has stayed the same

The original instinct that the student doing this for the first time, without guidance, in a Tier-3 town, with no one around who has been through it before, should have a fair shot has never changed.

Everything in the design flows from that. The single registration. The document vault that travels across counsellings. The guidance layer that explains what a seat offer means before the student has to decide. The alert that tells a specific student what they need to do next, not what the general population of students is doing.

The platform being built now is far more complex than what was imagined at the beginning. But the question it is trying to answer is the same one that started the work: why should navigating this process require a guide?
