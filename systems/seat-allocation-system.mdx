Seat allocation is a matching problem. The goal is not just to fill seats — it is to match the right student to the right institution. The quality of the match depends on the quality of the information and the design of the algorithm.

The current system optimises for process compliance. Superadmission optimises for match quality.

---

## The algorithm

Superadmission's allocation engine runs on the **Deferred Acceptance algorithm**, developed by David Gale and Lloyd Shapley in 1962 — work for which Alvin Roth received the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 2012 for its real-world applications in medical residency matching and school admissions.

Its core mathematical property: **no student can improve their outcome by misrepresenting their preferences.**

<Tip>
When the system is strategy-proof, students list what they actually want in the order they actually want it. No gaming. No coaching-class strategy. The algorithm does the rest.
</Tip>

## How the run works

<Steps>
  <Step title="Tentative matching begins">
    Every student is tentatively matched to their highest feasible preference. Capacity and category rules enforced at every iteration.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Iteration until stability">
    Students rejected from over-subscribed options move to their next preference. The process repeats until no student-institution pair exists where both would prefer each other over their current match.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Reserved seat recovery">
    Post-processing identifies under-filled reserved category seats and reallocates where rules permit. Seat utilisation is maximised before publication.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Validation before publication">
    Every allocation checked against a set of mandatory conditions. All must pass. If any fail, the record is quarantined — not the entire run.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Validation before any result publishes

| Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Merit cutoff | No student allocated beyond theirs |
| Double allotment | Zero tolerance |
| Reservation mandates | All satisfied |
| Identity anomaly | None permitted |
| Payment anomaly | None permitted |

<Warning>
Individual record fails: quarantined, authority notified, clean allocation published without the flagged record. Full run fails: allocation held, authority alerted within 60 seconds.
</Warning>

## Every outcome is explainable

This is the part existing systems do not do.

After every allocation run, every student gets a full decision trail:

<img src="/images/samplescreenshot.jpg" alt="Allocation audit trail — student view" />

- Which preference was tried first
- Which constraint blocked it (seat capacity, category limit, merit cutoff)
- Which seat was ultimately matched and why
- Which reservation rule was applied

A student who questions their outcome can see the exact logic. An authority defending an outcome has a complete audit log. A regulator checking compliance has a queryable record.

**Allocation that can be explained can be trusted.**
