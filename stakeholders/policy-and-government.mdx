India's higher education policy has set ambitious targets: 50% Gross Enrolment Ratio by 2035, multidisciplinary education, student mobility, credit portability, equity of access. These targets are well-reasoned and the national policy framework NEP 2020, NDEAR, APAAR, DigiLocker, the IndiaAI Mission has built many of the foundational layers needed to reach them.

What has not been built is the operational layer that connects these foundations to the moment a student actually tries to get into college. Superadmission is that layer.

This page is for policymakers, government officials, and institutional partners who are thinking about what national admissions infrastructure should look like and what it would take to get there.

---

## The policy case for unified admissions infrastructure

India's higher education system is adding scale faster than its admissions infrastructure can absorb. Since 2000, the number of higher education institutions has grown from roughly 10,000 to over 58,000, nearly a six-fold increase. Enrolment has grown from 8 million to 4.33 crore over the same period. NEP 2020's 50% GER target would add approximately 7 crore additional students, requiring the equivalent of 14 new universities every week until 2035 according to QS research.

The supply-side challenge; building institutions receives significant policy attention. The demand-side challenge; successfully moving students through admissions into those institutions receives considerably less. Yet the admissions infrastructure gap has direct consequences for the policy goals:

**Equity:** When process navigation advantage compounds merit, the system does not deliver on its equity commitments. Our research found that 67% of students rely on paid guidance to navigate what is a free public process. That gap between the stated promise and the actual experience is an admissions infrastructure failure.

**GER:** Students who exit the admissions process due to complexity or cost do not show up in GER figures. Our research found 12% of qualified students exiting before completing admission. At the scale of India's applicant base, this is a significant drag on enrolment numbers.

**Reserved category utilisation:** 23% of institutions in our research consistently struggle to fill reserved category seats. This is not a demand problem it is a coordination failure. Eligible students are not reaching the seats. This directly undermines the constitutional and policy commitments that the reservation system is meant to deliver.

**NEP implementation:** The credit portability and student mobility goals of NEP 2020 require that admissions connect to the academic record system. A student transferring between institutions under the credit framework needs their admission record to link to their APAAR and ABC accounts. Without an admissions layer that makes this connection, the mobility framework remains aspirational.

## What the data from Superadmission makes possible

One of the most significant policy implications of a unified admissions infrastructure is data. Currently, India's admissions system produces enormous amounts of information which students applied where, what the demand patterns were, where seats went unfilled, which categories were under-enrolled but this information is locked inside individual counselling portals and is not available in structured, cross-system form.

Superadmission produces, as a natural output of the admissions process:

- **Aggregate demand patterns** by state, stream, and category which combinations of eligibility and preference produce the highest demand, where demand consistently exceeds supply, where supply goes unclaimed
- **Equity analytics** - category-wise allocation rates, first-generation applicant outcomes, regional disparities in process completion rates
- **Vacancy analytics** - where seats go unfilled, in which categories, after how many rounds, and what the pattern looks like over time
- **Process analytics** - where students drop out of the process, at which step, and what the correlation is with socioeconomic background or geographic location

These are policy-relevant data sets that currently do not exist in usable form. A policymaker asking "where are reserved category seats consistently going unfilled, and what is driving that?" has no clean answer available today. After one full cycle on Superadmission, that question has a precise, data-backed answer.

## How Superadmission relates to federal complexity

India's higher education system is constitutionally shared between the Centre and states. Central counsellings (JoSAA, CSAB, NEET's all-India quota) operate under central authority. State quota counsellings operate under state authority. Private universities have their own admissions. This diversity is not going away, and it should not institutional autonomy and state authority over education serve important purposes.

Superadmission's design accommodates this directly. It is not a central system that state counsellings plug into and cede control to. It is a shared infrastructure layer like a protocol that each participating authority uses on their own terms. A state CET cell that integrates with Superadmission defines its own rules, runs its own rounds, and makes its own allocation decisions. What it shares is the identity and document verification layer, which benefits every authority that participates.

This federated model means that adoption can begin with a single state counselling and scale through demonstrated value, without requiring national coordination upfront.

## The DPI precedent

India's Digital Public Infrastructure model Aadhaar for identity, UPI for payments, DigiLocker for documents, ONDC for commerce has demonstrated a consistent pattern: shared, open infrastructure layers create more value than proprietary systems, and that value compounds as more actors participate.

UPI processed over 228 billion transactions worth ₹299.7 lakh crore in 2025. It handles more daily transactions than Visa. It did not do this by replacing banks it created a shared layer that banks plugged into.

Admissions is the sector where this model has not yet been applied. There is no UPI for higher education access. Superadmission is designed to be that built on the same India Stack foundations, following the same interoperability principles, governed with the same public infrastructure intent.

India championed DPI as a model at the G20 in 2023. Extending that model to the admissions sector would be a demonstration of DPI's breadth, not just its depth.

## What a government partnership looks like

Superadmission is a pre-revenue platform in active development. The prototype is functional. The first pilots are the immediate priority.

A government partnership at the central or state level could take several forms depending on the partner's role in the admissions ecosystem:

**State counselling pilot:** A state CET cell or state quota NEET counselling authority integrates Superadmission for a defined scope one stream, one round, or one category of seats to evaluate the platform's impact on verification overhead, grievance volume, and seat vacancy rates.

**Policy alignment partnership:** A government body responsible for education digitalisation or DPI development engages with Superadmission to align the platform's architecture with national standards and contribute to the policy framework for unified admissions infrastructure.

**Data partnership:** A policymaking body with interest in higher education equity or enrolment analytics engages with Superadmission to define what data the platform should generate and how it should be made available for policy use.

In all cases, the engagement starts with a conversation. The contact pathway is on the Engage With Us page.

## What we are not claiming

Superadmission is a pre-launch platform. The prototype works. The design is grounded in research. The alignment with national initiatives is genuine.

What we are not claiming:

- That national adoption is imminent or guaranteed
- That the platform solves all problems in higher education access
- That it replaces the policy and regulatory work that counselling authorities and the Ministry of Education need to do

What we believe: that India has built the infrastructure foundations for unified admissions, that the policy mandate for it exists, and that a working platform that demonstrates the value of that infrastructure will accelerate the policy alignment rather than wait for it.

The pilot is where that demonstration happens.
