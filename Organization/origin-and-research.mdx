The Superadmission architecture did not begin with a solution. It began with trying to understand why students applying to engineering and medical colleges in India were having such a difficult time with a process that, on paper, should be straightforward.

This page documents what was observed, what was studied, and what conclusions that research led to.

---

## How the Problem Was Encountered

The admission cycle following JEE and NEET results is a period of high activity and high stakes. What became apparent through direct observation was that a significant portion of the difficulty students faced was not about the complexity of their decisions — which college, which branch, whether to wait for an upgrade — but about the operational mechanics of participating in the process itself.

Students were spending hours on tasks that should take minutes. Uploading documents they had uploaded before. Tracking deadlines scattered across portals they had to check independently. Making high-stakes acceptance decisions under time pressure while simultaneously managing parallel processes in different systems.

The observation that motivated this project was simple: the student is being asked to act as the coordination layer between systems that should be coordinating with each other.

## What Was Studied

<AccordionGroup>

  <Accordion title="Student journeys">
    Multiple admission cycles were observed — not as statistics but as operational journeys. The specific tasks students performed, the points where errors occurred, the moments where deadlines were missed or nearly missed, and the decisions that were made under insufficient information. These observations shaped the problem framing before any solution was considered.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Existing counselling system architecture">
    The technical and operational structure of major counselling systems — JoSAA, MCC, and several state CET systems — was studied in detail. This included the registration flows, document requirements, allotment timelines, round structures, and grievance processes. The goal was not to criticise these systems but to understand what they do and do not do for the student.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="India Stack and digital public infrastructure">
    The architecture and design philosophy of Aadhaar, DigiLocker, UPI, and the Account Aggregator framework were studied both technically and in terms of their implementation trajectory. The ONDC model — a shared protocol layer for commerce — was examined as a reference for how shared infrastructure can enable coordination without requiring centralisation.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Comparable international models">
    Centralised admissions infrastructure in other contexts — UCAS in the UK, the Common App in the US, centralised medical admissions in other countries — was reviewed. These are not direct analogues to the Indian context, but they provide reference points for what coordination infrastructure looks like when it exists.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Policy and regulatory literature">
    NEP 2020, UGC guidelines, NMC regulations, DPDP Act, IT Act, and UIDAI regulations were reviewed for their implications on how an admissions infrastructure layer could be designed and eventually aligned with regulatory requirements.
  </Accordion>

</AccordionGroup>

## What Changed in the Thinking

Research produced several revisions to the initial framing. These are worth noting because they reflect how the architecture was shaped by evidence rather than initial assumptions.

**Initial assumption: the problem is a UX problem.**
Early thinking suggested that a better-designed portal — cleaner interface, unified calendar, better notifications — would meaningfully address the difficulty. Research showed this was wrong. Individual portal improvements do not change the structural problem: systems that do not share identity, documents, or status cannot be fixed by making each individual portal better. The problem is coordination, not design.

**Initial assumption: replacing counselling portals is the solution.**
A unified portal that replaces JoSAA, state CETs, and other systems was considered. This approach was rejected. Counselling authorities have built their systems over years and have specific operational requirements that cannot be absorbed into a single unified system without enormous political and institutional resistance. More importantly, it is unnecessary — the allocation logic and authority structures work. What is missing is the layer above them.

**Initial assumption: this requires a government mandate to work.**
Research into India Stack's deployment showed that voluntary adoption — driven by clear value — is a more realistic and more durable path than mandate-driven adoption. DigiLocker, UPI, and ONDC all grew through demonstrated value rather than compulsion. The proposed architecture is designed to work through voluntary authority participation rather than requiring a mandate.

**What remained consistent:** the framing that the student should not be the integration layer between systems. That observation has not changed. It is the architectural premise that everything else builds on.

## What Is Still Being Understood

Research is ongoing. The following areas remain active areas of inquiry:

- How state counselling systems vary technically and what integration would require in each case
- How counselling authority administrators experience the current system from their side — the operational load on authority staff that is less visible than student-side friction
- How document verification errors occur in the current system and what their frequency and consequences are
- How the proposed architecture would interact with management, law, and design admissions — streams that have different structures than engineering and medical
