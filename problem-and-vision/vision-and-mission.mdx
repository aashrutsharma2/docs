## Vision

**Every student in India reaches the right institution based on merit — not on their ability to navigate complexity.**

A first-generation student from a small town with a 97 percentile score should have the same shot as a student in a metro with a coaching centre, a consultant, and a parent who went to IIT. The same rank. The same outcome probability. No process advantage for those who already have advantages.

That is the vision. Not an aspiration. A design requirement.

---

## Mission

Build and operate the shared infrastructure layer for India's higher education admissions — connecting students, institutions, and counselling authorities through unified identity, verified documents, and auditable allocation.

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="For students" icon="user-graduate">
    Register once. Participate in every counselling you qualify for. Never upload the same document twice.
  </Card>
  <Card title="For institutions" icon="building-columns">

  </Card>
  <Card title="For authorities" icon="landmark">

## What success looks like

<Steps>
  <Step title="Near term">
    First live integration with a counselling authority. Real students, real cycle, real allocation. The prototype becomes a product.
  </Step>
  <Step title="3 years">
    Multiple state and central counsellings integrated. A student applying across engineering, medical, and state university counsellings manages everything from one profile.
  </Step>
  <Step title="10 years">
    Superadmission is to admissions what UPI is to payments. The infrastructure question is settled. Counselling authorities treat it as utility. Students do not think about it — it just works.
  </Step>
</Steps>

---

## The north star metric

One number captures whether the mission is being achieved:

> **The share of qualified students who reach a seat without paid external guidance.**

Today that number is approximately 33% — the inverse of the 67% who rely on paid consultants. If Superadmission works, that number rises toward the high nineties. Not because consultants are bad. Because the information and coordination they provide has been built into the infrastructure itself.

## What Superadmission does not aspire to

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Not a monopoly" icon="xmark">
    Counselling authorities retain full autonomy over their rules and allocation. Superadmission coordinates — it does not control.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Not a data business" icon="xmark">
    Student data is not monetised, not sold, not used for advertising. It is the input to the admissions workflow and nothing else.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Not a gatekeeper" icon="xmark">
    Participation for counselling authorities is optional. The platform creates no dependencies that are hard to exit.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Not a quick exit" icon="xmark">
    This is public infrastructure. It may take a decade to reach national scale. The founders chose this deliberately.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>
