---

India's higher education system is something worth appreciating before getting into where things break down. The examinations are rigorous. The evaluation is standardised. Results are trusted as a genuine measure of merit. Every year, crores of students sit for assessments that are conducted fairly, at national scale, across thousands of centres. That is a genuine achievement.

What hasn't kept pace is what happens after the results come out.

As of 2021-22, **4.33 crore students** are enrolled in higher education across India — that is more than the entire population of California, spread across 1,168 universities and over 43,000 colleges. In any given admission cycle, the students actively navigating counselling across streams runs well into the crores.

NEET UG 2024 alone saw **24 lakh students register**. JEE Main 2024 saw **14.76 lakh unique candidates**. Add MHT CET, KCET, AP EAPCET, TS EAMCET, WBJEE, and the dozens of other state-level examinations and the total picture becomes clear. India is running an admissions process at a scale that few countries come close to, without the unified infrastructure layer that countries running a fraction of this volume consider standard.

---

## How other countries handle it

The comparison is not meant to be unflattering. It is meant to show what the infrastructure gap actually looks like.

China's Gaokao; taken by over **13 million students annually**, making it the largest single examination in the world feeds into a nationally coordinated admissions system where students submit preferences centrally, scores are matched against seats province-wide, and the result is known within weeks of the exam. The entire sequence, from exam to confirmed admission, runs in roughly 6-8 weeks.

The UK runs all undergraduate admissions through a single platform, UCAS, handling over 700,000 applications per year. Australia does the same through state-based UAC equivalents. In both cases, a student applies once, documents are held centrally, and institutions receive verified candidate data.

| Dimension | India | China (Gaokao) | UK (UCAS) | Australia (UAC) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Unified application layer | No | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Student applies once | No | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Document verification centralised | Partial | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Typical exam-to-admission timeline | 4–6 months | 6–8 weeks | 6–8 weeks | 6–8 weeks |
| Seat vacancy rate (approx.) | 16–44%\* | Low | Low | Low |

\*Engineering, varies by year and state - AICTE data

India is operating at the largest scale. Its coordination infrastructure is the least centralised.

## The numbers behind the gap

**Document submissions**

Students submit documents ( caste certificate, domicile certificate, income certificate, marksheets ) an average of 4.2 times across their counsellings. Each submission enters a fresh verification queue taking 5 to 15 days. The information being verified is identical each time. Across crores of students and hundreds of counsellings, this is a significant volume of duplicated work that produces no new information.

**Seat vacancies**

Even in 2024-25 a strong year for engineering enrolments, with a record 12.53 lakh seats filled over **2.37 lakh engineering seats** went unfilled nationally after the vacancy rate dropped to 16.36% (AICTE). State-level numbers are sharper: Andhra Pradesh saw 1.2 lakh vacant seats, Tamil Nadu 54,587, Gujarat 30,000\+, all after multiple rounds of counselling.

In medical admissions, NEET 2024 produced **13.16 lakh qualified candidates** for approximately 1.96 lakh MBBS, BDS, and AYUSH seats. The seats and the students both exist. What's missing is the infrastructure to match them efficiently.

## The decade ahead

The National Education Policy 2020 sets a target of **50% Gross Enrolment Ratio** in higher education by 2035. India's current GER is 28.4% (AISHE 2021-22). Reaching 50% means enrolling approximately **7 crore additional students**; roughly a 61% increase over current enrolment.

QS research estimates that meeting this target would require India to add the equivalent of **14 new universities every week until 2035**. That is the supply side of the equation. The demand side successfully moving students through admissions into those institutions requires infrastructure that can scale with it.

The admissions infrastructure in place today was not built for 4.33 crore students. It will not absorb 7 crore more without structural change. The problem does not stay the same size while policy runs ahead of it.
